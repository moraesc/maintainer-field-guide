# 📚 Glossary of Open Source Terminology

> *"Every field has its jargon. Ours just happens to be particularly weird."*

A comprehensive lexicon for navigating the linguistic landscape of open source.

---

## A

**Abandonware** — Software whose maintainer has vanished into the digital ether, leaving behind a repo with 2,000 open issues and a README that says "actively maintained."

---

## B

**Bikeshedding** — Spending three hours debating whether a variable should be called `userCount` or `numberOfUsers` while a critical security vulnerability sits in the backlog. Named after Parkinson's Law of Triviality.

**Bus Factor** — The number of maintainers who would need to be hit by a bus before a project becomes unmaintainable. For most projects, this number is 1.

---

## C

**CHANGELOG** — A file that documents what changed in each release, theoretically maintained by humans, practically generated by scripts that parse commit messages nobody wrote carefully.

**CI/CD** — Continuous Integration / Continuous Deployment. The automated pipeline that tells you your code is broken 30 seconds after you push, instead of 3 days later when a user reports it.

---

## D

**Dependency Hell** — The state of having so many interdependent packages that upgrading any one of them breaks seven others. See also: "works on my machine."

---

## F

**Fork** — Creating your own copy of a repository, either to contribute back (good fork) or because you disagree with the maintainer's direction (drama fork).

---

## G

**GLHF** — "Good Luck, Have Fun." Occasionally seen in release notes for particularly risky upgrades.

**Good First Issue** — An issue labeled as appropriate for newcomers. In practice, it's either trivially easy or deceptively complex with no middle ground.

---

## H

**Hacktoberfest** — An annual event in October that incentivizes open source contributions. Known for causing a seasonal migration of Drive-By Typo Fixers. Has been both celebrated and cursed by maintainers.

---

## L

**LGTM** — "Looks Good To Me." The universal shorthand for "I glanced at this PR for 30 seconds and nothing caught on fire."

---

## N

**Nit** — A trivial code review comment, usually about formatting or naming. "Nit: trailing whitespace on line 47." The reviewer's equivalent of adjusting a painting that's 1 degree off-level.

---

## R

**Rebase** — The act of rewriting commit history to make it look like you knew what you were doing all along. Occasionally results in existential crises when conflicts arise.

**RFC** — "Request For Comments." A proposal document that invites feedback. The comments section often devolves into bikeshedding (see above).

---

## S

**Stale** — An issue or PR that hasn't had activity in a while. May be genuinely abandoned or may be patiently waiting for a maintainer who's been on vacation for six months.

**Squash** — Combining multiple commits into one, hiding the evidence of your 47 "fix typo" commits and the one "undo accidental deletion of entire database."

---

## W

**WIP** — "Work In Progress." A PR opened before it's ready, either to get early feedback or to stake a claim on the issue before someone else does.

---

## Y

**Yak Shaving** — The process of performing a series of increasingly tangential tasks to accomplish the original goal. "I needed to fix a bug, which required updating a dependency, which required upgrading Node, which required installing a new version of macOS..."

**YAGNI** — "You Ain't Gonna Need It." A design principle that says don't build features until they're actually needed. Universally acknowledged, rarely followed.

---

*This glossary is a living document. Like all living things in the open source ecosystem, it grows whether you want it to or not.*
